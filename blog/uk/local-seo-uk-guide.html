Local SEO UK: How to Rank in Google Maps and Local Results

Everything that drives local visibility for a UK business — Google Business Profile, citations, reviews, town-level page architecture, and a 90-day plan to build it.

For any UK business serving customers in a specific town, city, or region, local SEO is usually the highest-leverage form of SEO available — it competes for searches with clear buying intent from people close enough to actually become customers. This guide covers the full framework: Google Business Profile, citations, reviews, on-site architecture, schema, and how to sequence all of it. For the broader ranking picture beyond local, see our guide to ranking higher on Google UK and our general local SEO strategies guide. If you're a small or owner-run business, our SEO for UK small businesses guide covers how to prioritise this with limited time and budget.

Local Pack vs. Local Organic Results

UK local search results are typically made up of two distinct result types on the same page: the Local Pack (or Map Pack) — the block of three map-pinned business listings with photos, ratings and a map — and the local organic results beneath it, which are standard web page rankings that happen to favour geographically relevant pages. Google Business Profile completeness, review signals, and proximity dominate Local Pack placement, while content depth, backlinks, and on-page relevance dominate organic placement. A UK business that only optimises its website and never touches its Google Business Profile will often see decent organic rankings but a weak or absent Local Pack presence — and for most local searches, the Local Pack captures the majority of clicks, since it appears above the organic results and includes a map.

Understanding which result type a target keyword actually surfaces matters more than most businesses realise. Searching your own target terms from a clean, logged-out browser session shows what Google considers the dominant intent — heavily map-based results mean Local Pack signals should be the priority; a page of standard blue links with no map means organic content signals matter more. Most local commercial keywords in the UK ("plumber Leeds," "accountant near me") pull a Local Pack, which is why Google Business Profile optimisation is usually the fastest lever a local UK business can pull.

Setting Up Google Business Profile Properly

A complete, accurate, actively maintained Google Business Profile is the single highest-leverage asset in local SEO. That starts with verification — claiming and verifying the profile through Google's official process, using a genuine business address or defined service area — followed by filling out every available field: business description, opening hours (including bank holiday hours), attributes, services, and products. Incomplete profiles rank behind complete ones for otherwise similar businesses, all else being equal. See our dedicated Google Business Profile guide for field-by-field optimisation detail.

Beyond the initial setup, an actively maintained profile outperforms a "set and forget" one. That means uploading fresh, genuine photos regularly, publishing Google Posts for updates, offers or news, keeping the Q&A section monitored and answered, and updating hours and services as they actually change. Profiles that go quiet for months at a time tend to lose ground to competitors who are visibly active, both because Google's systems weight recency and because prospective customers can see the gap themselves when comparing listings.

Categories, Services and Service Areas

Primary category selection is one of the most consequential — and most commonly mishandled — fields on a Google Business Profile. Choosing the single most accurate, most specific category available (rather than a broader, more generic one) has an outsized effect on which searches a profile surfaces for. A business offering multiple services should still choose one primary category that reflects its main line of business, using secondary categories and the dedicated services section to cover the rest.

Service area settings need to reflect where a business genuinely operates, not an inflated radius designed to appear in more searches. Google has become more effective at identifying and penalising service-area claims that don't match real operating patterns. For businesses with a fixed premises customers visit (a shopfront, clinic, or showroom), the address should be the anchor; for service-area businesses (mobile trades, home services), the service area list should be an honest, specific set of towns or postcode districts actually served, not a blanket claim across an entire region.

NAP Consistency

NAP — Name, Address, Phone number — needs to match exactly across every place it appears: the website, Google Business Profile, every directory listing, and every citation. Inconsistencies (a slightly different business name, an old address, a disconnected phone number appearing on even one directory) dilute the trust signal citations are meant to provide, and can confuse Google's systems about whether two listings represent the same business.

The practical fix is a NAP audit: search the business name across major UK directories, note every place the details differ even slightly (abbreviated street types, an old suite or unit number, a former trading name), and correct each one systematically. This matters particularly for businesses that have moved premises, rebranded, or changed phone providers and never fully cleaned up the resulting inconsistencies across the directories where they're listed.

UK Citations and Directories

Citations — mentions of a business's NAP details on other websites, with or without a link — reinforce local relevance and legitimacy. For UK businesses, the highest-value citations tend to come from genuinely British, industry-relevant sources: trade and professional association directories, local chambers of commerce, and general-purpose UK directories such as Yell, Thomson Local, Scoot, and FreeIndex. A smaller number of high-quality, consistent citations from relevant sources outperforms a large number of low-value, generic, or geographically irrelevant ones.

Building citations is worth sequencing deliberately: start with the platforms most likely to be checked by both customers and Google (Google Business Profile itself, plus the two or three most authoritative general UK directories), then move into industry-specific and local directories relevant to the business's sector. Avoid low-quality directory networks that exist purely to sell citation packages with no genuine traffic or relevance — these add little value and occasionally attract spam-adjacent associations.

Acquiring Reviews Without Gating

Review volume, recency, rating, and response behaviour all factor directly into local ranking, particularly for Local Pack placement. The sustainable way to build them is a consistent, low-friction process: asking every customer (not a filtered subset) shortly after a positive interaction, with a direct link that minimises steps between the ask and the review being posted. A steady trickle of reviews over time outperforms an occasional burst followed by silence.

Review gating — filtering customers by sentiment before asking, so only likely-positive customers are prompted — violates Google's review policies and risks running against the UK's Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act rules on fake and misleading reviews, and carries real risk of profile suspension if detected. The compliant approach is to ask everyone, and to handle negative reviews through a genuine service-recovery process (a prompt, professional public response plus private follow-up) rather than trying to prevent them from being posted. Responding to every review — positive and negative — also signals an actively managed business.

Town and City Page Architecture

For businesses serving multiple towns or cities, dedicated location pages consistently outperform a single generic page that mentions several areas in passing — but only when each page is genuinely differentiated. A page built around a specific town should reference real local detail: the actual service area, any local context relevant to the service, and ideally genuine local case studies, project photos, or testimonials from customers in that area, not a templated page with only the location name swapped.

The failure mode to avoid is near-duplicate location pages — the same boilerplate copy repeated across ten town URLs with minimal unique content — which tends to cause the pages to compete with and dilute each other in search results rather than each ranking independently. A smaller number of genuinely distinct, well-built location pages will outperform a larger number of thin ones. For a business centred on London specifically, see our dedicated London page for how this plays out at the city level.

LocalBusiness Schema

LocalBusiness structured data gives Google an explicit, machine-readable summary of a business's name, address, phone number, opening hours, and service area, reducing ambiguity that Google's systems would otherwise have to infer from unstructured page content. It doesn't guarantee a ranking boost on its own, but it removes a category of uncertainty and can support richer search result presentation. The schema should exactly match what's visibly stated on the page and on the Google Business Profile — schema that contradicts visible content is a trust and accuracy problem, not a ranking hack, and can suppress rich result eligibility rather than earning it.

For multi-location businesses, each location should carry its own LocalBusiness entry with its own accurate address and phone number, rather than one schema block duplicated across every location page with only the town name changed. This mirrors the same principle as the page content itself: each location needs to be represented as genuinely distinct, both to users and to structured data.

Local Backlinks and Digital PR

Backlinks from genuinely local, relevant UK sources carry disproportionate weight for local ranking compared to generic or geographically irrelevant links. Realistic opportunities include local and regional press coverage (a genuinely newsworthy angle — a business milestone, original local data, community involvement), sponsorship of local events or sports clubs, partnerships with complementary local businesses, and guest contributions to genuinely relevant local or industry publications. Digital PR carries particular weight in the UK market, where regional press and industry trade publications are both active and genuinely link-friendly for well-pitched, newsworthy stories.

What doesn't work — and can actively hurt more than it helps — is generic, high-volume outreach for low-relevance links, or paid link schemes that violate Google's guidelines. A handful of genuinely local, genuinely earned links tends to outperform a much larger volume of low-quality ones, and the newsworthy-local-angle approach also tends to build actual brand awareness in the area, which reinforces the SEO benefit rather than existing purely for search engines.

Multi-Location Businesses

Businesses operating across multiple genuine UK locations face a structurally different local SEO problem than single-location businesses: each location needs its own Google Business Profile, its own accurate NAP details, its own dedicated page, and ideally its own review-generation process, rather than one shared profile or page trying to represent several locations at once. Google's systems are built around the assumption that a listing represents one physical or service-area location, and stretching a single profile across multiple genuine sites tends to underperform compared to properly separated ones.

The operational challenge scales with location count — consistent NAP details, consistent review-response cadence, and consistently non-duplicate page content across ten or twenty locations requires genuine process, not one-off effort. Centralising the underlying business information (correct hours, correct services, correct contact details per location) in one place that feeds every profile and page reduces the drift that causes inconsistencies to creep in over time as individual locations update independently.

Common Local SEO Mistakes

  • Inflated service areas. Claiming a radius or list of towns the business doesn't genuinely serve, which Google's systems increasingly detect and can penalise.
  • Duplicate location pages. The same content published across multiple town URLs with only the location name changed, causing pages to cannibalise each other.
  • Inconsistent NAP details. A different address, phone number, or business name across directories, diluting trust and confusing Google about listing identity.
  • Review gating. Filtering which customers get asked for reviews, which risks violating both Google policy and UK consumer protection rules.
  • Abandoned Google Business Profiles. Set up once, verified, and then never updated with fresh photos, posts, or accurate hours.
  • Schema that contradicts visible content. LocalBusiness markup listing different details than what's actually shown on the page or the Google Business Profile.

A 90-Day Local SEO Plan

Days 1–14 — Foundation. Claim and fully complete Google Business Profile (or audit and fix an existing one), correct the primary category and service list, run a NAP consistency audit across existing directory listings, and fix any discrepancies found.

Days 15–35 — Structure and citations. Build or refine location-specific pages with genuinely unique content, add LocalBusiness schema matching the profile exactly, and build citations across the highest-value UK directories relevant to the business's sector.

Days 36–60 — Reviews and content. Launch a consistent, compliant review-request process, begin publishing regular Google Posts and photo updates, and expand location page content with local specifics and, where available, genuine case studies.

Days 61–90 — Authority and measurement. Pursue two to three genuinely local backlink or digital PR opportunities, review ranking movement across target keywords, and identify which areas need another cycle of work.

FAQs

How long does local SEO take to work in the UK?

Google Business Profile and review changes can influence Local Pack visibility within four to eight weeks. Citation building and on-page local content typically take two to four months to show their full effect, particularly in competitive UK city markets.

Does my business need a page for every town it serves?

Only if you can build each one as genuinely distinct. A smaller number of well-built, differentiated town or city pages consistently outperforms a larger number of templated pages with the location name swapped, which tend to cannibalise each other in search results.

Is local SEO different in London compared to smaller UK towns?

The underlying mechanics are the same, but competition is usually far higher in London and other major cities, which means the same local SEO effort often produces faster, more visible results in a smaller town with fewer competing businesses.
