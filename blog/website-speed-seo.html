Website Speed and SEO

Page speed is a direct Google ranking factor and a direct conversion factor. Here's exactly how load time affects your rankings — and the fixes that make the biggest difference fastest.

Google has been explicit for years: page experience, and speed specifically, is a ranking factor. But speed matters even more for the metric that ultimately pays your bills — conversion rate. A slow website doesn't just rank lower; it actively loses you customers who never wait around to see your offer.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Industry research consistently shows bounce rates rising sharply as load time increases past two to three seconds, especially on mobile. If your site takes five or more seconds to become interactive, you're likely losing a majority of visitors before they see anything — regardless of how good your rankings or ad targeting are.

Core Web Vitals Are the Measurement Standard

Google measures speed through Core Web Vitals: Largest Contentful Paint (how fast the main content loads), Interaction to Next Paint (how responsive the page feels), and Cumulative Layout Shift (how visually stable it is while loading). These aren't vanity metrics — they're built directly into Google's ranking systems.

What Actually Slows Sites Down

In our audits, the same handful of culprits show up again and again: unoptimized images, render-blocking JavaScript and CSS, bloated page builders and plugins, and hosting infrastructure that isn't built for speed. Fixing these four areas resolves the vast majority of speed problems we encounter.

Image Optimization Is the Highest-Leverage Fix

Images are usually the single largest contributor to page weight. Compressing images, serving modern formats, and lazy-loading anything below the fold typically produces the fastest, most visible speed improvement of any single change you can make.

A Simple Test

Run your homepage through a free page speed testing tool. If your Largest Contentful Paint is over 2.5 seconds on mobile, that's costing you both rankings and conversions — and it's very likely fixable without a full site rebuild.

Speed Is a Foundation, Not a Feature

The businesses that treat speed as an ongoing discipline — monitored monthly, not fixed once and forgotten — are the ones that keep their Core Web Vitals scores in the "Good" range as content, plugins, and traffic grow over time. It's maintenance, not a project with an end date.
