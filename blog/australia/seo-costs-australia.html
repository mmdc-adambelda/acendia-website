SEO Costs in Australia (2026 Pricing Guide)

Real, sourced pricing ranges — what drives the difference between a $1,500 and a $6,000 monthly retainer, and how to compare proposals properly.

Quick Answer

Industry pricing guides published in 2026 commonly report Australian SEO monthly retainers in the rough range of AUD $1,000–$6,000+, with small local campaigns often landing under $1,500–$2,500 and competitive national or multi-city programs frequently exceeding $5,000. Some sources report average small-to-midsize business retainers clustering around $2,500–$3,500, with capital-city variation — Sydney and Melbourne retainers, for instance, have been reported averaging in the $3,700–$4,700 range. These are indicative figures from published industry sources (cited below, accessed 20 July 2026), not a quote — actual pricing depends heavily on your competition, scope, and goals, covered in detail below.

Why SEO Prices Vary So Much

SEO isn't a single, standardised service — a local single-location business and a national ecommerce catalogue require genuinely different amounts of work, so a single "market rate" doesn't really exist. Price differences between agencies usually reflect real differences in scope, seniority of staff involved, and how much technical, content, and authority-building work a campaign actually includes — not just arbitrary positioning.

Geography plays a role too. Reported average retainers vary meaningfully between capital cities — Melbourne and Brisbane figures have been reported somewhat higher than Adelaide or Perth in published industry pricing guides, reflecting differences in local competition and cost of doing business, not necessarily differences in the quality of work available in each city.

Common Pricing Models

  • Monthly retainer — the most common model for ongoing SEO, covering a defined scope of technical, content, and reporting work each month.
  • Project-based pricing — a fixed fee for a defined, time-limited piece of work, such as a technical audit or a site migration.
  • Hourly consulting — typically used for freelancers or specific advisory work rather than full campaign delivery.
  • Performance-based pricing — fees tied to ranking or traffic milestones; approach with caution, since it can incentivise chasing easily-gamed metrics over genuine business outcomes.

Most established agencies default to a monthly retainer, since SEO is inherently an ongoing discipline rather than a one-time deliverable — rankings that aren't maintained tend to decay as competitors keep investing. Project-based pricing suits a narrower, defined need, like a one-off technical audit ahead of a website rebuild, but rarely substitutes for ongoing work if sustained visibility is the actual goal.

What a Professional Campaign Should Include

Regardless of the pricing model, a professional SEO retainer should include: an initial technical and competitive audit, ongoing technical maintenance (crawl errors, schema, page speed), content strategy and production tied to real search intent, local SEO and Google Business Profile management where relevant, ethical authority-building, and a genuine monthly report tied to business outcomes, not just rankings. A retainer missing several of these — particularly technical maintenance or genuine reporting — is a narrower, lower-value service than the price might suggest.

Ask specifically how many hours of senior strategist time versus junior execution time your fee covers. Published pricing guides suggest a mid-range Australian retainer commonly allocates a modest number of hours to senior strategy and review each month, with the remainder going to hands-on technical and content execution — a useful benchmark when comparing whether a quote's hours genuinely match its price.

What Actually Drives the Cost

  • Competition level — a crowded capital-city commercial keyword set costs more to compete for than a lower-competition regional or long-tail niche.
  • Site size and technical debt — a large, technically messy site takes more hours to audit and fix than a small, clean one.
  • Number of locations — multi-location businesses need individually built local pages and citation profiles, which scales cost with location count.
  • Content and link-building intensity — more aggressive content production and authority-building both require proportionally more hours.
  • Reporting depth — genuine analysis and a real monthly conversation cost more to deliver than an automated export.

These factors compound rather than add in isolation — a multi-location business in a competitive capital city with significant technical debt sits at the upper end of the range on nearly every factor simultaneously, which is why national or multi-city retainers can run well above the figures typical for a single-location regional business.

The Risks of Cheap SEO

A price far below the rest of the market almost always means far less actual work — fewer hours, thinner content, minimal technical attention — rather than a genuine efficiency advantage. In the worst cases, unusually cheap SEO relies on tactics that risk a Google penalty: spammy link building, thin auto-generated content, or keyword stuffing. The cost of undoing that damage, plus the lost time, is often higher than what a properly scoped campaign would have cost from the start.

There's also an opportunity cost that's easy to overlook: months spent on an under-scoped, low-effort campaign are months a genuine competitor spends building real authority. By the time the underperformance becomes obvious, the gap to close has often grown, not stayed flat — which is part of why the cheapest quote is rarely the cheapest option once measured over a realistic time horizon.

How to Compare Proposals

Compare proposals on scope, not just the headline monthly figure — ask each agency to itemise what's actually included, and check that you're comparing genuinely equivalent scopes of work. Ask how many hours per month the fee covers, who specifically will be working on your account, and what deliverables you should expect to see within the first 90 days. Our guide to choosing an SEO company covers the broader evaluation framework this pricing comparison should sit inside.

Watch for proposals that quote a low headline price but bill separately for things a competitor's quote already includes — content production, technical fixes, or reporting, for instance. Ask directly: "is this the complete monthly cost, or are there items likely to be billed on top?" before comparing numbers side by side.

How to Estimate Return on Investment

A simple framework: estimate the value of a single converted lead or sale for your business, estimate a realistic number of additional leads a campaign could plausibly generate per month once it's established, and compare that recurring value against the monthly retainer cost. This won't be precise in month one — SEO's return builds over months, not immediately — but it gives you a sense of the breakeven point you're working toward, and a number to track against as the campaign matures.

Questions to Ask Before Signing

  • What specifically is included in this monthly fee, itemised by activity?
  • How many hours does this retainer cover, and who's actually doing the work?
  • What would you expect to have delivered within the first 90 days?
  • How does pricing change if scope increases — more locations, more content, more competitive terms?
  • What happens to pricing after an initial period — does it change, and under what conditions?
  • Are there any one-off setup fees on top of the monthly retainer, and what do they cover?

Write the answers down as you collect quotes — three months into an engagement is a frustrating time to discover a proposal implied something the contract never actually promised. A written scope of work, agreed before signing, protects both sides.

FAQs

How much does SEO cost per month in Australia?

Industry pricing guides commonly report monthly SEO retainers in Australia ranging from roughly AUD $1,000 to $6,000 or more, with small local campaigns often under $1,500 and competitive national programs frequently above $5,000. These are indicative ranges from published industry sources, not a fixed quote.

Why is SEO pricing so inconsistent between agencies?

Because the scope of work varies enormously — competition level, site size, technical debt, number of locations, and content and link-building intensity all affect how much time a campaign genuinely requires, and pricing should reflect that scope rather than a flat, one-size-fits-all rate.

Is cheap SEO worth it for a small Australian business?

Rarely for anything beyond very light maintenance. A price far below the market typically means far less actual work — fewer hours, less technical depth, thinner content — which tends to produce correspondingly limited results, or in worst cases, tactics that risk a Google penalty.
