SEO Company vs Freelancer vs In-House Team in Australia

Three genuinely different delivery models, each right for a different situation. Here's how to work out which one actually fits your business.

SEO can be delivered three genuinely different ways — an agency, a freelance consultant, or an in-house hire — and each is a legitimately good choice in the right situation. The mistake most Australian businesses make isn't picking the "wrong" option in some absolute sense; it's picking based on price or convenience alone, without weighing the trade-offs below against their actual size, complexity, and goals.

This guide compares the three models on the dimensions that actually matter — not to steer you toward one particular answer, but to give you an honest framework for a decision that genuinely depends on your specific situation. A ten-person local trades business and a hundred-person national ecommerce brand can both make the right call for themselves and land on different answers.

Quick Comparison

  • SEO agency — full team covering technical, content, local, and reporting; higher cost, fastest full-scope capacity, built-in continuity.
  • Freelancer — one person, usually strongest in one or two disciplines; lower cost, more flexible, but limited capacity and a single point of failure.
  • In-house hire — full-time, deeply embedded in the business; highest fixed cost, slowest to build full skill coverage, but the most control and institutional knowledge over time.

Best Fit by Business Size

A small, single-location business with a modest budget is often best served by a focused freelancer or a smaller agency engagement — the scope doesn't yet justify a full-time hire or a large agency retainer. A growing business competing across multiple locations or a genuinely competitive national keyword set usually needs the full skill coverage an agency provides, since technical, content, local, and authority-building work all matter simultaneously. A large business with SEO as a core, ongoing growth channel is where an in-house hire — sometimes alongside an agency for specialist work — starts to make sense.

Revenue and growth trajectory matter more than current size alone. A small but fast-growing business planning to compete more aggressively in twelve months' time may be better served starting with an agency relationship it can scale, rather than a freelancer relationship it will likely outgrow and need to replace at exactly the point momentum is building.

Skill Coverage

Modern SEO spans technical auditing, content strategy, local SEO and Google Business Profile management, digital PR, and analytics — genuinely more disciplines than one person typically masters at a high level. An agency's advantage is having different specialists for each; a freelancer's advantage is depth in the one or two areas they've specialised in, at the cost of the others; an in-house hire's skill coverage depends entirely on that one person's background, which is why the role often needs support from contractors or tools to cover the gaps.

Before committing to any model, honestly map which of these disciplines actually matter most for your business right now. A local service business might get 80% of its value from Google Business Profile and local content work, in which case a specialist freelancer in exactly that area can outperform a generalist agency team spreading effort across disciplines that matter less to that specific business.

Cost Structure

A freelancer is typically the lowest hourly or project cost, but capacity is limited to their available hours, and complex technical or content work may need separate outsourcing, which narrows the apparent savings. An agency retainer costs more per month but bundles a full team's capacity, which is often better value once you account for the specialist skills it would otherwise take multiple freelancers to replicate. An in-house hire has the highest fixed cost — salary, superannuation, tools, and management overhead — but that cost is stable and predictable regardless of how much work is actually needed in a given month, which can cut either way depending on your workload.

Compare total cost of ownership, not just the headline rate. A freelancer's day rate might look cheaper than an agency's monthly retainer on paper, but factor in the cost of your own time spent managing and coordinating the work, the tooling you may need to buy separately, and the cost of covering gaps in disciplines they don't specialise in — the real comparison is rarely as simple as the two headline numbers.

Speed and Capacity

An agency can typically move fastest on a broad initial push, since multiple specialists can work in parallel — a technical audit, content production, and local SEO setup happening simultaneously rather than sequentially. A freelancer's capacity is capped by one person's available hours, which can create a bottleneck during a busy period. An in-house hire's capacity is also capped at one person initially, but with the advantage of full-time, undivided attention to your business specifically, rather than attention split across multiple clients.

Accountability

An agency has institutional accountability — processes, management oversight, and typically a documented reporting cadence, plus continuity if an individual staff member leaves. A freelancer's accountability is entirely personal and relationship-based, which works well with a reliable, communicative freelancer but has no institutional backstop if that relationship breaks down. An in-house hire reports directly to you, which offers the most direct control, but also puts the full burden of evaluating their work on you or another internal manager who may not have deep SEO expertise themselves.

This last point is worth taking seriously: if nobody internally can tell good SEO work from mediocre SEO work, an in-house hire's output can drift for months without anyone noticing — the same blind spot that makes evaluating an agency or freelancer difficult applies just as much to managing an employee.

Tooling

SEO work depends on a stack of paid tools — rank tracking, technical crawlers, keyword research platforms, backlink analysis — that cost real money regardless of who's doing the work. An agency typically spreads this tooling cost across multiple clients, making it a rounding error in your retainer. A freelancer may or may not have a full tooling stack, depending on how they've built their practice. An in-house hire usually means the business absorbs the full tooling cost itself, on top of salary — often several hundred dollars a month in subscriptions that are easy to underestimate when budgeting for a new hire.

Risk and Continuity

A freelancer or a single in-house hire both carry the same structural risk: if that person is unavailable, unwell, or leaves, work stops or slows until a replacement is found and brought up to speed. An agency's team-based structure means one person's absence doesn't halt the engagement, and institutional knowledge about your account persists even through staff changes on the agency's side.

This risk compounds with account access. If a freelancer or in-house hire set up accounts under their own login rather than the business's, losing that person can mean losing access entirely, not just losing momentum — another reason the ownership and access terms covered in our guide to choosing an SEO company matter regardless of which delivery model you pick.

The Hybrid Delivery Model

Many Australian businesses land somewhere between these three pure options: an in-house marketing generalist who owns the relationship and coordinates strategy, paired with an agency or freelancer handling the specialist technical and content execution. This can combine the control of an internal owner with the skill coverage and capacity of external specialists — worth considering explicitly rather than defaulting to an all-or-nothing choice.

Decision Checklist

  • Does your SEO need span multiple disciplines (technical, content, local, authority) at once, or is it narrowly focused on one?
  • Is your budget better suited to a flexible, lower hourly cost, or a bundled monthly retainer?
  • Do you have someone internally capable of evaluating specialist SEO work, or do you need an accountable external team?
  • How much does business continuity matter if a single individual becomes unavailable?
  • Is SEO a core, ongoing growth channel for your business, or a defined project with a start and end?

FAQs

Is a freelancer cheaper than an SEO agency in Australia?

Usually on a per-hour basis, yes, but not always in total value delivered. A freelancer typically covers fewer disciplines than an agency team, so complex technical or content work may need to be outsourced separately, narrowing the cost gap.

When does it make sense to hire in-house for SEO?

Generally once SEO is a large enough part of the business that a dedicated person can stay fully utilised, and once the business has the internal capability to manage and evaluate that hire's work. Below that threshold, an agency or freelancer is usually more efficient.

Can I use a freelancer and an agency at the same time?

It can work if responsibilities are clearly divided — for example, a freelancer handling content while an agency manages technical SEO — but it requires clear coordination to avoid duplicated or conflicting work.
