SEO Audit Checklist for Australian Websites

Every category a proper SEO audit should cover, what to check in each one, and how to prioritise what you find.

An SEO audit is only useful if it's specific enough to act on — a report that just says "improve your content" or "build more links" isn't an audit, it's a restatement of the problem. This checklist is built to be genuinely usable: concrete items across five categories, with guidance on which issues to fix first when you find several at once.

Why Run an SEO Audit

Most websites accumulate SEO problems gradually — a redirect added and forgotten, a page's meta description left as a placeholder, a Google Business Profile category that made sense years ago but no longer matches the business. An audit surfaces what's actually true about a site right now, rather than what was true when it was originally built, which is why even a well-performing site benefits from a periodic audit rather than only auditing sites that are visibly struggling.

An audit is also the right first step before starting any new SEO work, including everything covered in our guides to ranking number one on Google Australia and local SEO in Australia — it establishes a genuine baseline and identifies which of the many possible improvements will actually move the needle for a specific site, rather than working through a generic priority list that may not match the site's real problems.

Technical SEO Checklist

Technical issues are often invisible to users but directly affect whether Google can crawl, index, and understand a site correctly. Check: whether key pages are actually indexed (via Search Console's URL Inspection tool), whether the sitemap.xml is current and submitted, whether robots.txt is blocking anything it shouldn't, whether HTTPS is correctly enforced sitewide, and whether Core Web Vitals scores are in the "Good" range on both mobile and desktop. Also check for redirect chains (a URL that redirects to another redirect, rather than directly to the final destination), broken internal links, and duplicate content caused by URL parameters or www/non-www inconsistency. Our technical SEO guide covers each of these in more depth.

Also worth checking: whether canonical tags are set correctly and self-referencing on pages that don't have a genuine duplicate elsewhere, whether structured data validates without errors (a schema block that fails to parse is silently ignored by Google rather than causing a visible error, so it needs an explicit check), and whether the site's XML sitemap only includes canonical, indexable URLs rather than a mix of redirects, non-canonical variants, and pages blocked by robots.txt — a sitemap padded with URLs that shouldn't be there can quietly waste crawl budget on larger sites.

On-Page SEO Checklist

On-page issues are usually the fastest to find and fix. Check that every important page has a unique, accurate title tag and meta description — not left blank, not duplicated across multiple pages, and not auto-generated in a way that doesn't reflect the page's actual content. Check heading structure: one H1 per page that accurately describes the content, with H2s and H3s used to organise sections logically rather than for styling. Check that images have descriptive alt text, that internal linking connects related pages sensibly, and that each page has a clear, singular purpose rather than trying to target several unrelated topics at once.

It's worth checking title tag and meta description length specifically — titles that run long enough to be truncated in search results, or descriptions that get cut off mid-sentence, lose the persuasive framing they were written with. Also check that each page's title and H1 aren't identical word-for-word; having them closely related but not duplicated gives Google two related but distinct signals rather than one repeated one. Finally, check for keyword cannibalisation directly: search the site (using a `site:` search or Search Console's performance report) for a target keyword and confirm only the intended page is genuinely competing for it.

Local SEO Checklist

For any business with local intent, this category is often where the most impactful, fastest-to-fix issues are found. Check Google Business Profile completeness (category accuracy, services, hours, photos, recent posts), NAP consistency across the website, GBP, and any existing directory listings, and whether LocalBusiness schema is present and matches what's visibly stated elsewhere. Check the review situation: volume, recency, average rating, and whether the business is actually responding to reviews. For multi-location businesses, check that each location has its own properly differentiated page and its own GBP listing rather than a shared, generic setup. See our full local SEO Australia guide for the complete framework behind each of these checks.

Also check the service area setting against genuine operating reality — an inflated radius or suburb list that doesn't match where the business actually serves customers is both a policy risk and a trust issue. And check for stale information specifically: opening hours that haven't been updated for a public holiday period, services listed that are no longer offered, or photos that no longer represent the current premises or team — all of which are easy to overlook once a profile has been set up and left alone.

Content Checklist

Check for thin or outdated content: pages with very little substantive text, pages that haven't been updated in years despite covering a topic that's changed, and any pages competing with each other for the same keyword (a sign of accidental cannibalisation rather than a deliberate content strategy). Check whether the site's content actually matches search intent for its target keywords — a page written as a sales pitch when searchers are looking for genuine information will struggle regardless of how well-optimised it otherwise is. Check for content gaps: real questions the target audience has that the site doesn't currently answer anywhere.

Check freshness signals specifically for any content where currency matters — pricing information, statistics, regulatory or industry information that changes over time. A page that was accurate two years ago but hasn't been reviewed since can quietly become a liability rather than an asset, both for user trust and for how Google assesses the page's ongoing relevance. Where a "last updated" date is displayed, confirm it's genuinely accurate and not just cosmetically bumped without a real content review behind it.

Off-Page Checklist

Check the backlink profile for red flags — a sudden spike in low-quality or irrelevant links (which may indicate a past negative SEO attempt or a poor-quality link-building service used previously) and genuine opportunities being left unclaimed (relevant local citations the business isn't listed on, industry directories it qualifies for but hasn't joined). Check brand mention consistency and whether the business's NAP details are accurate everywhere it's referenced online, not just on the primary citation sources.

Also check for broken or lost backlinks — links that used to point to the site but now 404, either because a page was removed without a redirect or because the linking site changed its own URL structure. Recovering these (via a redirect to the most relevant current page, or by reaching out to update the link) is often faster and cheaper than earning new links from scratch, and it recovers authority that was already earned rather than requiring new outreach.

How to Prioritise What You Find

A typical audit turns up more issues than can realistically be fixed at once, so prioritise by a combination of impact and effort: fix free, high-impact items first (an incomplete Google Business Profile, missing title tags on key pages), then move to higher-effort but still high-impact items (technical fixes, content gaps on money pages), and treat lower-impact polish items (minor alt text gaps, small formatting inconsistencies) as ongoing maintenance rather than urgent work. A long list of minor issues is far less urgent than a small number of foundational ones — don't let checklist length substitute for genuine prioritisation.

Free and Paid Audit Tools

Google Search Console and Google Business Profile Insights cover a genuine share of this checklist at no cost — indexation status, Core Web Vitals, search performance, and GBP activity are all available for free directly from Google. Google's PageSpeed Insights covers Core Web Vitals in more detail. Beyond the free tools, a single mid-tier paid SEO platform can speed up backlink analysis, keyword tracking, and site-wide technical crawls, but for most small-to-medium Australian businesses, the free tools cover the majority of what this checklist requires.

A browser's own developer tools are also worth using directly for a manual spot-check — viewing page source to confirm a title tag or canonical URL is actually rendering as intended, rather than relying solely on a third-party tool's interpretation of it. This matters particularly for sites with any client-side rendering, where what a tool reports and what Google actually sees can occasionally diverge, and a manual check is the most reliable way to confirm what's really being served.

When to Get a Professional Audit

A DIY audit using this checklist is genuinely sufficient for many small businesses, particularly for the on-page and local categories. A professional audit tends to earn its cost when technical issues are suspected but hard to pin down (unexplained ranking or traffic drops, a site that seems to have crawl or indexation problems without an obvious cause), before a significant site migration or redesign, or when a business needs a second, expert opinion before committing to a larger SEO investment. FAQs

How often should a business run a full SEO audit?

A full audit once or twice a year is usually enough for most small-to-medium businesses, with lighter monthly checks (Search Console errors, ranking movement, review activity) in between. A major website change, migration, or a sudden ranking drop should trigger an out-of-cycle audit regardless of schedule.

Can I run an SEO audit myself, or do I need a professional?

The on-page, local, and basic technical checks in this checklist are realistically DIY-able with free tools like Google Search Console. Deeper technical issues — crawl budget problems, JavaScript rendering issues, complex redirect chains — often benefit from professional tools and experience to diagnose accurately.

What's the single most common issue found in Australian small business SEO audits?

Incomplete or inconsistent Google Business Profile setup paired with missing or duplicated title tags and meta descriptions — both are foundational, both are free to fix, and both are frequently left unaddressed while attention goes to content or backlinks instead.
